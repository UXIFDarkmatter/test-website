Why we exist

Top-down
mandates aren't
working.

California's communities are not interchangeable — and the state's own data says we don't need 2.5 million more units.
— Department of Finance Projections, flat through 2060

A vision of California created by special interest groups claims we have a housing crisis at all levels, and forces development into our communities regardless of fire risk, infrastructure, transit access, or actual job locations.

The true crisis is a lack of housing affordable to lower-income families. But almost all of the forced development is luxury — at a steep cost to our environment, our infrastructure, and our safety.

Streamlining laws fast-track huge projects without impact studies, community input, or long-term planning. The state is more interested in unit count than evacuation capacity.

Wake Up California believes lasting solutions require affordable homes, local stewardship, and public accountability. Cities should retain the authority to plan — not just process applications.

Six things you should know

Did you
know?

The numbers don't add up. The narratives don't match the data. Here's what the state isn't telling you.

01
Mandate

No need for 2.5 million units.

The need to house 10 million more residents is unsupported — the methodology behind that number failed audit.

02
Affordability myth

Supply doesn't create affordability.

Housing prices don't trickle down. Building more luxury housing doesn't lower costs. We aren't building enough of the right type.

03
Definitions

"Low income" units for people making $100K.

Cities can't require developers to build truly affordable housing — so they build luxury, and call it "low income" by definition.

04
One-size-fits-all

Why densify the whole state?

Mandates are dumped on every locality regardless of infrastructure, hazards, transit, water supply, or job creation.

05
Fire risk

Densification in hazard zones can be deadly.

Increasing density in fire hazard areas means structure-to-structure spread, like we just saw in the Palisades.

06
Hidden cost

City resources are being drained.

Just the paperwork to satisfy state laws costs localities hundreds of thousands — even millions — that won't go toward housing.

— What is this based on?

The YIMBY /
Abundance
agenda.

An astroturf strategy — not a movement.

The legislation reshaping California — SB 9, SB 35, SB 423, SB 79 — is presented as a grassroots response to a housing crisis. It isn’t.

It’s the policy product of a coordinated YIMBY / Abundance agenda — an astroturf strategy that successfully recruited disaffected younger Californians who can’t find a job or a home, and pointed their frustration at neighborhoods, zoning, and CEQA.

The Sleight of Hand

Frame neighborhood preservation as the cause of the housing crisis. Then sell the legislative cure — which happens to deliver every concession the development industry has wanted for thirty years.

The cure is engineered to benefit large developers, institutional investors, and corporate landlords — at direct cost to communities, environmental review, fire safety, local democracy, and the families already living in California.

The result is predictable. Luxury units labeled affordable. Single-family homes consolidated into corporate portfolios. Cities forced to absorb compliance costs while losing the authority to plan. Fire-prone neighborhoods densified without evacuation studies. The actual housing crisis — affordability for working families — left untouched.

Wake Up California exists to name the strategy, expose the funders, and rebuild a politics where housing policy serves communities, not portfolios.

Many fields, one warning

It isn't just one kind of expert
raising the alarm.

There isn’t a single reason to think we’re living through bad policy decisions that will damage the state we love. The case against current housing policy is being made by safety officials, economists, environmentalists, planners, preservationists, architects, and affordable-housing advocates — each from their own discipline, all pointing the same direction.

i
Public Safety

Fire & evacuation advocates

Adopted standards exist on the books — and are being ignored. Density mandates collide with evacuation realities in fire-prone communities.

ii
Economists

The numbers don't add up.

The 2.5 million unit mandate failed audit. State population projections are flat through 2060. Supply alone doesn’t produce affordability.

iii
Preservationists

Streamlining erases place.

Historic resources, neighborhood character, and treasured public spaces are being demolished by fast-track laws that bypass local review.

iv
Environmentalists

CEQA was removed, not reformed.

Environmental review has been gutted in the name of speed. Wildlife corridors, watersheds, and open space are being sacrificed.

v
Planners

Local plans overridden.

Decades of careful general plans, zoning, and community-developed visions are voided by state preemption — replaced with one-size-fits-all density.

vi
Local Government

Cities pay; the state collects credit.

Localities absorb hundreds of thousands — even millions — in compliance costs. They face fines and receivership but receive no funding.

vii
Architects

Bad design at scale.

Streamlined approvals produce identical luxury blocks regardless of context — disconnected from climate, terrain, light, or human use.

viii
Affordable housing

Luxury, labeled "affordable."

"Low income" units are being priced for households making $100,000+. Cities can’t require true affordability, so developers build what pencils.

ix
Families & Neighborhoods

SFH isn't racist — it's universal.

Families across every culture want backyards, neighborhoods, room to raise kids. We don’t need to destroy the neighborhoods we love to get the housing we need. Eliminating single-family homes doesn’t democratize them — it concentrates ownership in large corporate landlords, locks ordinary families out of building wealth, and makes the homes that remain available only to the rich.

x
Climate

Concrete & heat islands.

Mass concrete construction is among the highest-emission building methods. Densified urban cores intensify heat islands precisely as the climate warms.

xi
Equity

TOD is ageist & ableist.

Transit-oriented development assumes everyone walks, bikes, and tolerates extreme heat. In a warming world with intense weather, that assumption fails the elderly, disabled, and vulnerable.

The pattern
When safety officials, economists, environmentalists, planners, and affordable-housing advocates all reach the same conclusion from different starting points — that’s not a coincidence. That’s a policy failure.
What good policy looks like

Better outcomes
start with better questions.

i

Communities shape their own growth.

Local residents understand fire and evacuation realities, infrastructure limits, treasured historical resources, environmental constraints, and community needs. Cities should retain the authority to plan — not just process applications.

ii

Count affordability first.

The state assigns unachievable unit targets and twists the numbers. A studio, a five-bedroom house, and a fifty-bed care facility all count as one "unit." Affordability isn't part of the total count.

iii

Developers must be accountable.

If private entities are granted public power, they must participate in community solutions, provide enforceable affordability, and share long-term responsibility. Profit should follow public benefit, not replace it.
